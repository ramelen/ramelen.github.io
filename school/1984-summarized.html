1984 Independent Novel Study

Nineteen eighty-four is a very strange book. It was written in 1949 by Eric Arthur Blair, more commonly known as George Orwell, and it is a cross between a social science fiction and a dystopian alternate earth. The main character’s name is Winston Smith, who was born in the superstate Oceania in the city Airship One, which today would be known as london. Winston works in the Ministry of Truth “correcting errors'' in past records. He secretly hates the party, and knows that he will be tortured and killed because of it. He falls in love with a coworker, Julia, and after a lengthy affair, they decide to join a revolution against the party. They meet Mr. O’Brien, who sets them up in the revolution, but soon betrays them and both Winston and Julia end up in the ministry of truth, where they are tortured and presumably killed.

The first thing that I liked about this book was that the concepts that were explored and the themes (total surveillance, nationalism censorship, and inequality, among others) were very appealing to me, so the parts where the author writes about the system of Ingsoc, or english socialism and how it functions in a book that Winston reads, and one of my favourite lines was: “A peace that was truly permanent would be the same as a permanent war. This—although the vast majority of Party members understand it only in a shallower sense—is the inner meaning of the Party slogan: War is Peace” (Poe, Part 2, Chapter 9). This moment in the book really clicked in my head the reasons why the party does what it does, to uphold a core value. That value isn’t a very good one, but the thing that makes the party tick finally makes sense. It really makes the story that much more plausible. The other thing that I really liked about the book that had to do with the world building was the final scene, where Winston is at the Chestnut Tree Cafe, where he says the very last line of the book, “He had won the victory over himself. He loved big brother” (Poe, Part 3, Chapter 6). This was another powerful moment for me because it made me very confused at first about whether this was a good ending or not. After thinking about it for a while, I decided that this was a good ending, which gave me a laugh because I had won the mini battle over myself, and loved big brother. Anyway. I liked how the author answered all of my questions about the system of the society.

I didn’t love everything about this book however. I think that it could have had some more humor to add an ironic twist to the style. Speaking of the style, the way the story was written kept me captivated, yet it was quite draining to read, because the style is very melancholy and harshly real. This is a section of the book that was picked at random: “But actually, he thought as he re-adjusted the Ministry of Plenty’s figures, it was not even forgery. It was merely the substitution of one piece of nonsense for another” (Poe, Part 1, Chapter 4). Winston is reflecting on the reality of truth in the party, in a very detached way. He could have said something like “As he altered the figures in the day newspaper, he thought about how the figures that he was correcting were most likely wrong, and there was no shame in changing wrong numbers to equally wrong numbers”. This puts things in a more positive light. I do understand that this was to drive the message to the reader, but I found it a little hard to read at times. One ther complaint that I have was that most of the characters weren’t developed as much as I wanted them to. Winston and Julia have plenty of character development, but the other characters are much more flat compared to them.

Overall, I would give this book a double thumbs up and I would recommend 1984 to anyone who enjoys reading unique perspectives, and I would not recommend it to anyone that doesn’t like sad stories.
