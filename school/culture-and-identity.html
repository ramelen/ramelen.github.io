Culture and Identity

Culture is a very curious thing. It arises when people come together and interact for a long time, and their interactions become more and more strange the more they talk. Culture however, affects more than just how you talk to other people, it also deeply changes the way that you think about yourself. There are many ways that culture affects your identity, but first we must understand how culture is expressed.

There are many different ways that humans can show other humans what’s on their mind. For one, we can just talk. Talking is one of the most I have met people that spend quite a lot of time on social media, and they use vocabulary that reflects their online culture, like “lol” or "subscribe". Some people talk very fast and often because they are part of a culture that doesn't value silence. Another more subtle way to express culture is to create things, like art or literature, making music about our daily woes or how love is so hard. Culture results from the way that prople interact with each other, sonit only makes sense that culture can also arise from how people interact with the world around them. The stuff that people think about is often related to their environment, because they need to pay attention to it to live. When major things happen, people change the way that they think, and thus the way that they express their culture. First nations culture was very nature oriented because they spent all of their time hunting, crafting clothes, or preparing food. They didn’t think about math or philosophy all that much because they didn’t need to, and so their culture addressed the things that they did do, and all the things that they would need to know. In places with a big oil industry like Alberta, people are much less supportive of climate action because it means that their way of life has to end. They spend their whole day possibly working in the industry, and they would all lose their jobs if the oil industry was shut down. People are more hostile because they were raised by oil workers who needed to be or else they wouldn’t get paid. The oil in Alberta fundamentally changes the way that people view other people. There are even weird things that happen when cultures interact with other cultures.

Several things can happen when cultures meet. They can acknowledge or ignore each other, like most cultures, or they can assimilate into a larger culture, like what happens when two people from India and Canada have a child, and that child grows up with both an Indian and a Canadian culture. One of the cultures can try and destroy the other one, like in Germany where Jewish people were brought into work camps and then killed. Similarly, One can suppress the other, and try to put their own culture into the other people, which was the purpose of residential schools, to save the barbaric children by teaching them christianity and civility. This however can create a new culture, one of rebellion and Cultures sometimes change drastically after coming into contact with another culture.

So what does this mean for me? From a young age, I was labeled as smart, and I wasn’t really being challenged at school. I was reading a lot of adult books, and I had quite a lot of knowledge from reading books like “homo sapiens” in grade four, so I naturally watched a lot of educational videos when I could be on youtube. I had a wide variety of educational content available to me, and I was watching it all. I was a part of this culture that valued truth and knowledge. I also had an academic dad, and the things that he liked talking about were either work or cool engineering concepts, so I picked the latter and he was feeding into my culture as well. Additionally, because I spent most of my time in school, I had a use and a need for lots of this knowledge, because we were learning it. At this point, I had been a nerd for over half my life and it has had a huge impact on the way that I think, the friends I make, and how I ask questions.
