The Fur Trade

The Fur Trade is a system of trading where first nations would hunt mammals like beavers for their pelts, and they would trade the fur to Europeans for things like guns, horses, and tools. The Europeans would then ship the pelts to Europe to be turned into hats and robes as a status symbol of high society. Most of this took place in Eastern Canada (The West coast did get some love, though not till later.) from the 16th century to the late 19th century. The fur trade is still happening today, just not as strong as during the colonization of North America. Speaking of colonization of North America, The whole event changed so many lives and the world views of people across continents. The Europeans had been living in Eurasia for hundreds of thousands of years, so they had developed much larger populations and societal ideals than the First Nations because they had only been developing their technology and economy for a mere ten thousand years. They still believed in animism, and people were still living in small tribes where everybody knew each other - compared to the bustling cities and complex competitive markets of Britain and France.

One of the largest changes in First Nations lives was the shift in economy and world view from a world created to share and live in harmony to a much more capitalistic landscape. Because Europe always needed more pelts, the first nations could focus just on animals like beavers to provide a huge income of supplies and tools, than they had before. This disrupted the ‘use the entire animal’ philosophy of the previous generations and was dropped in favor of high profit margins and huge payouts. They were spending so much time hunting for fur that they didn’t have very much time to hunt for food. They created borders and increased the amount of conflict between tribes over border disputes, even though never would have before.

As with many economic revolutions, There were more people, and they had more stuff, but they had a whole new set of problems bundled along with that. In certain areas, it wasn’t the Europeans that were drainting the land of beavers, but the first nations were doing more damage to the environment. It wasn’t all them though, some of this trading involved products from Europe that killed thousands of First Nations people. It was Europe that introduced alcohol to the trading ports, and that caused rampant alcoholism all over North america. The Europeans also had extremely deadly diseases that they were carrying around, and that ended up killing 90%, or 50 million of the first nations population. As with many economic revolutions, the long term consequences were much worse than the short term benefits.

So, to round things up - the short term consequences of the fur trade were good. The first nations were wealthier than ever before, and business was booming in Europe too, but the good times wouldn’t last. The Europeans thought it would be a good idea to get the first nations hooked on alcohol, like they did with China and opioids. They also decided that because the first nations were clearly inferior and almost animals, they started killing them using plague blankets mixed in with normal ones for trading. They ended up killing most of the first nations, and they put the survivors in residential schools. They also brought the idea of capitalism and made the first nation less respectful of their own tradition. So the main effect for the first nation people is that they lost 90% of their population plus permanent societal dysfunction and the Europeans got fancy hats.
