Have Empires Led to a More Peaceful World?

Empires are a great force of progress, driving humanity forwards ever on. They improve the lives of many people, and allow humans to cooperate in numbers unachievable otherwise. However, this cooperation has a disastrous downside; it causes frequent and devastating wars. It seems that just when humans are at their greatest, are they the most blood thirsty and hateful. In 10000 BCE, the majority of the population lived in small groups along the coast, spending their whole life in support of a small number of people, all of whom they knew deeply. When they made a decision, it was with every person in mind. This level of thoughtfulness and empathy meant that conflict was very rare, and when it did arise, it was often dealt with non-violently and amicably (Lomas 2).

After the advent of agriculture, the population exploded, and soon enough large villages became cities of thousands, and managing resources couldn’t be done by group consensus, like before. To handle decisions, leaders were appointed, which meant that the manpower of a city could be much easily coordinated, but it also meant the wisdom of the crowd was no longer accessible. Leaders had to be skeptical of anything that other parties did. The first commonly accepted empire in the Akkadian empire. At its height, it was ruled by one king, Sargon, and he used the resources of a large united population to try and conquer the known world (Salem Media, 2). During his 50 year reign, there were 34 wars. Before that, there was no civilization coordinated or populated enough to make this possible.

The other factor in war is amount of force that each party can muster. As the population increased, the severity of wars increased because instead of a village of 50 raiding another village, it was the Akkadian empires standing army of 5400 raiding the entire countryside (Salem Media). In the case of two nations warring, each side would have had this size of army, which would have had disastrous consequences for the population of each nation. In an increasingly hostile world, conflict was no more a quarrel between brothers, but a desperate fight to maintain the livelihood of every citizen in a kingdom.

Wars continued to get more frequent and devastating, boosted occasionally by revolutionary discoveries such as gunpowder, or the steam engine. This culminated in the World Wars, when a relatively small conflict called on alliance after alliance until almost the entire world was involved (History.org). The military power of the entire world was being used by the empires of that time in a fight for survival, and it is estimated that this caused over 100 million deaths, both of civilians and soldiers (Britannica). Since then, the severity and amount of wars have gone down to record lows, in part because people do not want a repeat of the world wars, and in part because of the existential threat of nuclear weapons, which is so great that many countries have banned their use. Additionally, the fear that these weapons have caused has brought together countries to form the largest alliance in the world (world101).

Historically, empires have made the population more susceptible to war, and in general, the larger the popuation in a group, the less peace that they will have. However, empires are the only way to achieve enormous levels of growth in a very short time, and we seem to be moving towards more peace in the future as world leaders form larger and larger alliances. Perhaps empires will be beneficial in the long term if not in the short term.
