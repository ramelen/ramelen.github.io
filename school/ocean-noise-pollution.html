Consequences of Science Pertaining to Ocean Noise Pollution

When you think of the ocean, you may imagine serene waters, filled with life, free from the dangers of hurricanes and forest fires. This is not the case. Sound, which travels extremely far in water and is the primary sense of almost all marine life, is being emitted by humans in enormous quantities. This is causing confusion and death almost everywhere in the oceans. Why is it here, and what can we do about it?

Sound in the oceans before the industrial revolution consisted of two things (Conlon). The first is biophony; animal communication like whale calls and dolphin chirps. This is a vital part of these species' lives, used for everything from mating to coordination. The second is geophony; storms, hydrothermal vents, cracking ice, earthquakes, and gas bubbles. This makes the background sound level in the ocean about 100dB.

On top of this, ships all over the world create large amounts of noise if their engines are touching the hull, or if the propeller makes low pressure zones as it spins, generating a constant hum that we cant hear, but that results in a constant rumble in a large area around the boat. Rumbling can also be caused by drills in oil rigs, or by low flying airplanes (Beane). Military sonar technology emits startlingly loud bursts regularly, so these bursts can be heard by sensors on the other side of the earth. All this can raise the sound level to almost 200dB.

This is of course hurting the very sensitive ears of marine life, who’s ears have been tuned to take advantage of the range of sound. Near constant noise can deafen and confuse whales, as well as taking away time for them to talk to each other. This is also known to cause masking, where an animal loses the ability to detect sounds of interest, like mating calls or prey sounds. The noise interferes with essential processes, which has led to huge numbers of deaths and even mass beachings (Marsh) after military tests of sonar equipment (Marsh). Sudden noises, like those from air guns used to find oil, can cause hemorrhaging and decompression sickness as the animal swims up and away from the sound too fast source?.

Sustained elevated sounds seem to cause lower biodiversity (Weilgart), and lower fishing yields due to fish avoiding the area altogether. Noise around whales may compound on other issues, leading to more occurrences with net entanglement and ship strikes, and destroying already polluted sources of food. Several technologies have been developed to solve this issue. Propellers can easily be redesigned to produce less noise by reducing the low pressure areas under a blade which create bubbles (Beane). Adding sound insulation into ships is another way to reduce the amount of noise emitted. Another technology is called Vibroseis (Marsh), which is orders of magnitude quieter than current oil survey methods. Restrictions have been put in place to restrict the amount of noise in some areas, and it has been shown that whales are less stressed when there are less ships in an area, like after 9/11 and during the beginning of Covid-19 (Schiffman).

These solutions seem like plenty enough to reverse this process, and the nice thing about noise is that as soon as you stop making it, it goes away. This is good because we have more important issues, so we can afford to solve this issue more slowly. On the other hand, if this goes on for too long, there might be irreversible damage to biodiversity or the ecosystem.
