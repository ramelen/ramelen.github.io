The Collapse of the Wari Empire

The Wari Empire formed around the 6th century in the Andes, and through an aptitude to expansion and a strong economy, became the first nation in South America to be centrally governed. They survived through a thirty year drought that brought down the earlier Moche and Nazca civilisations, and built an extensive road network to rule over their territory. They became so influential as to affect art and architecture styles of the incas. Six centuries later, the people of Wari had mostly disbanded into separate polities. The question still remains, How did the Wari empire collapse, and why?

The Wari had been the first to use a terraced farming system, which allowed them to utilise the hills as farmland, advanced canal systems allowed them to channel rain water to cities at the foot of the hill, and be very efficient with water use. This allowed the empire to both withstand drought and to introduce maize as a staple food alongside the already common tubers. With the excess workforce, Wari warriors would search for polities and conquer them, either by force or, often agreeing to let the rulers retain power in exchange for labour in the capital. They would bring canals and roads to many places, and the standard of living increased for many people that joined the Wari. At peak influence, much of the coastal and highland areas were part of the empire.

The Wari were special in how they used this power, because most of the affairs were handled in the capital city, which was the first occurrence of this happening in South American history. This was in part made possible by the widespread use of psychedelics, where lavish parties would be held with psychedelic beer, to relax the attendees and make them more likely to act in favour of the empire. The political control this allowed was a very powerful tool. This was the first time that a government was administered from just one place, and it allowed them to make more effective decisions and increase their power much faster than if each tribe had acted on its own. The Tiwanaku were like this, they were a group of polities that had shared a belief system and would fight for each other or lend help if needed. The government in Wari was stable for now, but under powerful stresses, it would not survive.

At around 950 CE, a global climate change occurred called the Medieval Climatic Anomaly. For most of the Americas this meant centuries of higher temperatures and lower amounts of precipitation. There was a lot of strain on the agriculture in the Andes, as the efficient water canals couldn’t deal with prolonged drought. This is thought to be the cause of the collapse of the Tiwanaku, as they didn’t have the agricultural technology that The Wari did. Even they were barely making it by. It was here that the political control that the Wari had was stretched thin, and there was a social and governmental breakdown. This was the main cause of the Wari collapse. Indiscriminate and systematic violence broke out. Lizzie Wade writes: “Even in their heyday, the Wari were no strangers to violence … [b]ut as long as the empire was strong, the violence was ritualized and limited.” (Wade, p.5) She goes on to talk about the amount of people with both non-lethal and lethal head injuries, which skyrocket up to three times what they were at the peak of The Wari Empire.

Overall, the reign of the Wari was a successful one, advancing technology and governance in South America, and paving the way for the Incan empire to form. It is also a good exercise in the fall of empires, how and why they happen. It is not perfect, because another category of stress on a nation are the external events, such as sudden innovation or military advance. The only problem is that studying history is that it is going to come with a lot of uncertainty, and sometimes outright missing information. South America didn't have any kind of writing system during the reign of the Wari, so anything that can only be learned from writing can't be deduced from ruins and skeletal remains. The names of important people or gods are lost to time, and the trade relations of the Wari with the Tiwanaku have not been proven, but given current evidence and reasoning, many people agree about the fate of the Wari. The current story in my mind is that an extended period of drought caused increased political tensions which eventually shattered the unstable government.
